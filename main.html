Visualizing climate change mitigation ... $_\widetilde{\odot}\Pi_{\Xi\bar{\odot}}$

Stocker shows both carbon and inaction affect global temperature. In his model: (1) carbon ($\text{CO}_2$) emissions (per year) increase exponentially until some chosen date and then decrease exponentially; (2) peak warming is proportional to the total (cumulative) carbon emissions. The model is suitable for ball-park engineering design and for guiding climate policy.
Stocker, Thomas F., "The Closing Door of Climate Targets" Science, vol. 339, no. 6117, pp. 280-282, 18 January 2013. *"The linear relationship between cumulative carbon emissions and global climate warming implies that as mitigation is delayed, climate targets become unachievable."*
If $C_\infty$ are the total cumulative carbon emissions, then the global peak warming is $$ \Delta T = \beta C_\infty $$ where $\beta$ is an empirical factor found from simulations. Here we assume $\beta=0.002^\circ\text{C}/\text{GtC}$. Other values can be selected in the code. *Enable the "Coffee" section to see the details of the computation.*
Say $E(t)$ is the global anthropogenic emissions (in gigatonnes per year) at year $t$; $E_0$ = 9.3 GtC/yr are the emissions at time $t=t_0=$ 2009; $C_0$ are the cumulative emissions at $t_0$; $r$ is the rate of emissions increase per year until $t=t_1$; $s$ is the rate of emissions decrease after $t_1$ (due to some Global Mitigation Scheme (GMS)). Then, before the GMS $$ E(t_0 < t \leq t_1)=E_0 e^{r(t-t_0)} $$ And, after the GMS $$ E(t>t_1)=E_0 e^{r(t_1-t_0)}e^{-s(t-t_1)} $$ The cumulative anthropogenic carbon to time $t>t_0$ is $$ C(t)=C_0+\int_{t_0}^{t}E(\tau)~d\tau $$ and the total is $C_\infty=C(\infty)$. In this presentation we assume piece-wise linear behavior prior to $t_0$. This is outside Stocker's paper, but we note his comment "The exact path of the emissions before $t_0$ is not important here ...". *Enable the "Coffee" section to see the details of the computation.*
As an analogy, imagine your car ($_\widetilde{\odot}\Pi_{\Xi\bar{\odot}}$) creates *all* the anthropogenic carbon in the world, and the total carbon depends on the distance you travel. The further you go, the hotter it gets (run the simulation again). The faster you go the sooner you get there.
The global mean temperature increase is proportional to the total cumulative carbon (from the model) and so proportional to distance (in the analogy).
If distance is carbon, then speed is the carbon emissions ($E(t)$ in GtC/yr). That is, the amount of anthropogenic carbon created per year. If emissions are higher it gets warmer sooner.
Initially all is well (green light) and you accelerate vigorously (yellow light). Then things go awry, and in 2009 you see a red light. *Your decision is when and how hard to brake*. Brake later and you must brake harder (before reaching a given temperature). Pass a point on the road (i.e., temperature) and no amount of braking can take you back (Stocker's "closing door"). Click the green "GO!" button and years begin to pass and you're soon at the yellow light. After the red light (year 2009) *click in the blue rectangle* to select a braking level (0%-10%), and see the ultimate effect on global temperature. Hover the mouse for more details.
"Braking" corresponds to the introduction of a Global Mitigation Scheme (GMS) in Stocker---causing an exponential decay in the emissions from the point of braking ($t_1$) onwards.
